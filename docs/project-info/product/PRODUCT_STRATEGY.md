# Carelog — Product & Business Strategy

## The problem

Caregiving coordination is invisible labor. When an aging parent needs care,
families cobble together group texts, shared Google Docs, and phone calls.
Professional aides show up without context. Siblings argue about who's doing
more. Doctors don't know what happened between appointments. The primary
caregiver burns out silently.

There is no single source of truth. There is no coordination layer. There is
no tool that serves the full care team — family, aides, and supporters together.

## The opportunity

**3.5 million CareZone users were abandoned.** Walmart acquired CareZone in 2020,
then shut down Walmart Health in 2024. These users were active, paying, and suddenly
homeless. They know what good medication management looks like. They're searching
for an alternative right now. This is the most concrete short-term acquisition
opportunity in the market.

**The competitive field is thin:**
- CaringBridge — communication only, nonprofit, buggy
- Lotsa Helping Hands — volunteer coordination only
- Caring Village — closest competitor, shallow execution
- CareZone — abandoned (Walmart acquired 2020, shut 2024)

No one owns the full stack: scheduling + medications + journal + documents +
finances + caregiver wellbeing. That's the gap.

## The product thesis

One subscription. One source of truth. One platform for the entire care team.

The care team includes: a coordinator (usually a family member), caregivers
(siblings, spouse), professional aides, and supporters (friends, extended family
who want to help but aren't daily caregivers).

Each role sees a different view of the same data. The coordinator has admin controls.
The supporter gets the weekly digest and can react to journal entries. The aide
logs their shift and the medication they gave. Everything ends up in the same
longitudinal record.

## Pricing

- **Family plan:** $14/mo or $120/yr
- One subscription covers the whole care team
- Unlimited team members per org
- Free tier: limited (to be determined — possibly single user, no history export)

No per-seat pricing. No per-feature gating beyond free vs paid.
The family pays one bill and everyone on the care team gets access.

## Business model

Bootstrapped. No VC. No marketing budget.

Revenue comes from:
1. Direct family subscriptions ($14/mo)
2. Eventually: employer benefits partnerships (ianacare model)
3. Eventually: home care agency licensing

The employer channel is year 2+. Focus year 1 is entirely on direct family acquisition.

## Go-to-market strategy

### No paid advertising except one exception

The one exception: $200/mo Google Ads on "CareZone alternative."
This is targeted, high-intent, finite-window. Everything else is earned.

### Channel priority

**1. Personal network (Month 1)**
Get 5-10 families from people you know personally. Not to pitch — to listen.
Offer free access for 6 months in exchange for brutal honesty.
Every early family who loves it sends three more.

**2. Professional referrers (Month 2-3)**
These are the highest-leverage distribution channel:
- Hospital discharge planners (see 5-10 families/week in crisis)
- Social workers (NASW — National Association of Social Workers)
- Elder law attorneys (families formalizing care arrangements)
- Geriatric care managers (GCMs — manage 10-20 families at once)
- Hospice workers
- Pharmacists

These people recommend tools to families in crisis. They're motivated by client
service, not commission. One referrer who sends 2 families/month is worth more
than 1,000 social media followers.

**Do not offer commission to social workers** — it conflicts with their professional
ethics and makes them uncomfortable.

**3. Content/SEO (Month 2+, compounds for years)**
Target high-intent caregiving searches:
- "What to do in first 72 hours after parent discharged from hospital"
- "How to talk to siblings about sharing caregiving responsibilities"
- "CareZone alternative"
- "How to divide caregiving responsibilities between siblings"
- "Caregiver burnout signs"

One genuinely excellent 1500-word article per week. Not SEO-stuffed.
Articles that social workers will bookmark and send to families.

**4. Online communities (Start now)**
- Reddit: r/AgingParents, r/dementia, r/CaregiverSupport
- Facebook groups for Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, ALS caregivers

Rule: be genuinely helpful for 3 months before mentioning the platform.
Answer questions honestly. Build reputation as someone who knows caregiving
coordination before being known as the founder.

**5. CareZone displacement landing page (Immediate)**
- Dedicated page: "Looking for a CareZone alternative?"
- Honest comparison with other options including ours
- Free medication import tool (let them upload CareZone export)
- $200/mo Google Ads on "CareZone alternative" only

### GTM sequence

```
Month 1:   Personal network (5-10 families) + communities + CareZone page
Month 2-3: Professional referrers (local — Chicago first)
Month 4-6: Content acceleration + referrer expansion (3 more cities)
Month 6-12: Referrer resource kit + PR pitches + first employer pilot
Year 2:    Institutional + employer channels
```

### Referrer maintenance

Referrers forget. Monthly touchpoints that are genuinely useful — not sales:
- One piece of caregiving information they can share with families
- Note when you ship a feature that directly addresses something they mentioned
- Handwritten thank-you when they send their first referral

## Key metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Month 1 | 5 active families |
| Month 3 | 25 active families |
| Month 6 | 100 active families |
| Month 12 | 500 active families |
| Referral rate | 60%+ of new families by month 6 |
| Time to first care event | Under 10 minutes |
| Week 4 retention | 70%+ |
| Monthly churn | Under 3% after month 3 |
| Active referrers | 10 by month 6 |

The referral rate is the most important single metric. If below 40%, the product
isn't generating word of mouth — that's a product problem, not a marketing problem.

## The four real moats

### 1. Accumulated family data (strongest, grows with time)
After 6 months, a family has hundreds of journal entries, medication history,
symptom trends, shift records, handoff notes. That data is irreplaceable.
Recreating it means recreating months of lived caregiving experience.
Every day the family uses the platform, switching cost grows.

**How to reinforce:** Make data depth visible. Show "You've logged 847 care events
over 8 months." Make the history export excellent — families who know they can
leave anytime are more likely to stay.

### 2. Multi-person coordination lock-in (very strong)
Most software is used by one person who can switch unilaterally. This platform
is used by an entire care team. Switching requires convincing all of them
simultaneously, migrating roles, re-onboarding aides, re-establishing context.
Every additional team member raises the switching cost for everyone else.

**How to reinforce:** Design for larger teams. Every additional person is a
moat-deepening action. Track team size per family as a KPI.

### 3. Professional referrer relationships (takes time, hard to copy)
A social worker who has recommended the platform to 50 families has professional
reputation invested in it. A competitor can outspend on ads the day they launch.
They cannot replicate two years of referrer relationships in any amount of time or money.

**How to reinforce:** Treat referrers as long-term relationships. Monthly value,
not monthly pitches.

### 4. Trust with a vulnerable population (fragile but powerful)
Trust in a caregiving platform is qualitatively different from trust in a
productivity tool. Families share health data, family conflict, financial
information, and end-of-life wishes. That trust takes years to build and can
be destroyed by one bad experience.

**How to reinforce:** Radical transparency. Pricing changes announced 60 days early.
Security practices visible. Data export always available. No dark patterns. Ever.

### The compounding loop

More data → more trust → more team members → more data

Referrers bring families who start accumulating data immediately.
The moats compound together. No single moat is impenetrable. All four together
are very hard to displace.

## What threatens the moats

1. **Well-funded startup copies the product** — they can take new families,
   cannot easily take families already using ours. Speed of acquisition year 1
   is moat construction.

2. **Trust violation** — data breach, misleading pricing change, feature removal
   that loses records. This is the existential threat. One bad experience destroys
   years of built trust.

3. **Founder sells to wrong acquirer** — CareZone's failure mode. Fix: stay
   bootstrapped and profitable, or raise only from aligned investors. Publish a
   public data stewardship commitment early.

4. **Product stagnates** — brand without product quality is just awareness of
   something people used to use. Keep shipping.

## Public data stewardship commitment (to publish before first users)

"If this platform ever shuts down:
- We will give families 12 months notice
- Full data export will always be available
- Your data will never be sold
- We will never run ads in this product"

Making this commitment publicly makes it harder to renege and builds enormous
trust with a population that has been burned before (CareZone).

## Competitive differentiators

vs. CaringBridge:
- We coordinate, they communicate
- Scheduling, medication tracking, aides — none of that exists in CaringBridge
- Natural response to "how is this different?": "CaringBridge is for updates,
  we're for coordination. A lot of families use both."

vs. Caring Village:
- Deeper execution on every feature
- Professional aide support (shifts, coverage requests, handoffs)
- Burnout tracker
- Financial coordination
- Shift-based scheduling for mixed care teams

vs. CareZone (what it was):
- Full care team coordination (CareZone was individual + family)
- Scheduler with professional aides
- Burnout awareness layer
- End-of-life planning
- Data portability (CareZone's data was hard to export)

## What we're stealing from competitors and why

Features identified from competitor analysis that were incorporated into the design:

**From Caring Village:**
- Outer circle — volunteer board for friends/neighbors
  (people want to help but don't know how)
- Care brief — shareable point-in-time summary for new providers

**From CaringBridge:**
- Supporter reactions on journal entries
  (lightweight acknowledgment without full commenting)
- Emotional journal framing — write to the person receiving care,
  not about them

**From ianacare:**
- Employer benefits channel (year 2)
- Coverage request board
- The "helpers don't know how to help" insight

**From Lotsa Helping Hands:**
- Outer circle task/slot model
- No-account-required claiming (share_token approach)

**From Apple Health / structured health records:**
- Normalized medications table (not jsonb blobs)
- Prescription label scanning → structured data
- Shareable care summary (care brief)
